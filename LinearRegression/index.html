🔭 Quick Check: Why does data that spans many orders of magnitude cause problems for standard linear regression?
The algorithm cannot converge when values are very large.
A linear model forces a constant additive slope, but the relationship changes multiplicatively — small changes at low values are ignored while large values dominate.
You need at least 100 data points to handle wide data ranges.

Part Three

Log-Log Regression: The Key Transformation

Start from Kepler's Third Law, and take the natural logarithm of both sides:

Kepler's Third Law T² = (4π²/GM) · a³
After log transformation ln T = ½ ln(4π²/GM) + 3/2 · ln a
β₀ (intercept, encodes G)      β₁ = 1.5 (predicted slope)

On the log-log scale, Kepler's law is a straight line with slope exactly 3/2 = 1.5. This is a powerful insight: nonlinear power laws become linear when we transform the variables.

Interpreting Log-Log Coefficients

LINEAR-LINEAR  β₁ = change in y per unit change in x (additive)

LOG-LINEAR   β₁ = fractional change in y per unit change in x (semi-elasticity)

LOG-LOG      β₁ = % change in y per 1% change in x (elasticity / power law)

Here: a 1% increase in orbital radius → 1.5% increase in orbital period. Double the radius (100% increase) → 21.5 ≈ 2.83× longer period.

📐 In a log-log model, if β₁ = 3, a 10% increase in x corresponds to what change in y?
A 30% increase in y.
A 3× increase in y.
Approximately a 33.1% increase in y — because 1.1³ ≈ 1.331, so y scales by ≈ 1.331×.

Adding the Second Predictor: ln(M)

Return to the physics. Taking logs of Newton's full formula:

Log-linearised Newton's Law ln T = ½ln(4π²) − ½ln G − ½ln M + 3/2 · ln a

Extracting G from the Intercept

The intercept β₀ encodes the gravitational constant G:

β₀ = ½ ln(4π²/G) = 14.39  ⟹  G = 4π²/e²⁸·⁷⁸ ≈ 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²

We measured the gravitational constant by fitting a regression. Kepler found the pattern. Newton explained it. Regression quantified it.

Why β₂ is Negative: Intuition

β₂ = −0.5 means: heavier central body → shorter period. Jupiter's immense gravity whips Io around in just 1.77 Earth days. More mass means stronger gravitational pull, faster orbit.

Coefficient Stability Analysis

This is one of the most underused tools in applied regression. When you add a new predictor, watch what happens to existing coefficients:

→
Coefficients barely move — new predictor is independent of existing ones. Safe to include or exclude.
⚠
Coefficients shift substantially — confounding. The new predictor is correlated with old ones and "stealing credit." Interpret carefully.
✗
New coefficient small, p large — variable adds no explanatory power. Drop it.

The F-Test for Groups of Predictors

To formally test whether a group of predictors improves the model beyond chance:

F-test Statistic F = (RSSreduced − RSSfull) / q ÷ RSSfull / (n − p − 1)

An Interaction Term: Does the Radius Effect Depend on Mass?

Newton's law is a clean product: T ∝ a3/2 × M−1/2. On the log scale, this becomes a sum — meaning there should be no interaction term. The effect of ln a on ln T should not depend on ln M.

But suppose we didn't know this. Could we test it from data?

Interactions Beyond Physics

In any multiple regression, if you suspect "the effect of X₁ depends on the level of X₂," that's an interaction. You test it by adding X₁·X₂ as a feature.

💼 Does the effect of advertising spend on sales depend on market size?

💊 Does the effect of drug dosage depend on patient age?

📚 Does the effect of study hours depend on prior knowledge?

All are mathematically identical to testing ln(a) · ln(M).

🔗 On a log-log plot, what does it mean visually if an interaction term is significant?
The data points will scatter more widely around the regression line.
The regression lines for different groups (e.g., light vs. heavy central bodies) will be non-parallel — they will converge, cross, or fan out, indicating different slopes.
The intercept will shift but the slope will remain constant across groups.

Our final model: ln T = β₀ + β₁ ln a + β₂ ln M. Now we verify it meets the assumptions of OLS regression.

VIF ≈ 1 for both predictors — virtually zero multicollinearity. Orbital radius and central body mass are independent physical properties. You can have a small moon orbiting a giant planet at any distance.

Why log transforms improve diagnostics: On the log scale, a given relative error (e.g., ±2% in period measurement) produces a constant absolute error in ln T, regardless of magnitude. Log transforms convert multiplicative errors (common in physical and economic systems) into additive, constant-variance errors.

⚠️ Physical outlier: Hyperion (Saturn's moon) shows the largest deviation due to its unusually high eccentricity (e = 0.123) and chaotic tumbling rotation. It's an outlier for physical reasons we can now understand.

🔍 A residual vs. fitted plot shows residuals that fan out as fitted values increase (trumpet shape). What regression assumption is violated?
Linearity — the functional form is wrong.
Independence — the observations are correlated.
Homoscedasticity — the variance of residuals is not constant across fitted values (heteroscedasticity). A log transform often fixes this for multiplicative-error processes.

Part Eight

Summary: The Complete Regression Journey

Let's trace every step of the modelling process and what each one taught us.

The Code: Putting It All Together

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# Data from the solar system table
df = pd.DataFrame({'object': ['Mercury', 'Earth', ...],
                   'a': [0.387, 1.000, ...],
                   'T': [0.241, 1.000, ...],
                   'M': [1.000, 1.000, ...],
                   'e': [0.206, 0.017, ...]})

# Log-transform the variables
df['log_a'] = np.log(df['a'])
df['log_T'] = np.log(df['T'])
df['log_M'] = np.log(df['M'])

# Model 1: Raw scale (terrible)
m1 = smf.ols('T ~ a', data=df).fit()        # R² = 0.71, residuals awful

# Model 2: Log-log, planets only
planets = df[df['M'] == 1.0]
m2 = smf.ols('log_T ~ log_a', data=planets).fit()   # slope ≈ 1.500 ✓

# Model 3: Two predictors, all 20 objects
m3 = smf.ols('log_T ~ log_a + log_M', data=df).fit()
# β₁ ≈ 1.499, β₂ ≈ -0.501, intercept encodes G ✓

# Model 4: Add eccentricity (insignificant)
m4 = smf.ols('log_T ~ log_a + log_M + e', data=df).fit()
# p(e) = 0.62 — drop it

# Model 5: Add interaction (also insignificant)
m5 = smf.ols('log_T ~ log_a * log_M', data=df).fit()
# Newton's clean product confirmed — no interaction needed

Philosophical Musings

Kepler found the 3/2 power law by inspection.

Newton derived it from first principles.

We recovered it — along with the gravitational constant — by fitting a two-column design matrix to 20 rows of data and reading the coefficients.

Regression doesn't just fit lines. It extracts hidden meaning.

🎓 Final Synthesis: Why is the OLS formula β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy equivalent to "rediscovering the law of universal gravitation" in this context?
Because Newton used matrix algebra when deriving his laws.
Because the formula minimises squared error, which is more physically meaningful than other loss functions.
Because when X contains [1, ln a, ln M] and y = ln T, the OLS solution finds coefficients that encode both Kepler's exponents (β₁ = 3/2, β₂ = −1/2) and the gravitational constant G in β₀ — extracting physical law from raw data alone.
